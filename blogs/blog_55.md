# International Trade Compliance Checklist for Exporters

Blog 55 of 200

UK exporters in 2026 are operating in the most complex regulatory environment since the pre-WTO era. Post-Brexit licensing rules, evolving sanctions regimes, new customs procedures under the UK Border Target Operating Model, and the shifting import requirements of key export markets — the EU, US, and Middle East — have all changed in the past three years. For many mid-sized manufacturers and trading companies, the internal team that used to handle compliance under EU rules has not kept pace with what post-Brexit international trade compliance actually requires.

The result is predictable: delayed shipments, unexpected duty costs, failed customs entries, and in the worst cases, penalties from HMRC or the Export Control Joint Unit. None of these outcomes are inevitable — they are the result of running an export operation without a current, properly documented compliance framework.

This post gives UK exporters a practical international trade compliance checklist for 2026 — covering customs documentation, sanctions screening, export licensing, and how to stay compliant when shipping to EU, Middle East, and US markets. Use it as an audit tool against your current processes.

## Why UK Trade Compliance Has Changed Fundamentally Since Brexit

The shift from EU membership to standalone UK trade policy has not settled. UK trade compliance 2026 still involves working through the consequences of a trading relationship with the EU that changed completely in January 2021 and has continued to evolve since. The UK Border Target Operating Model, which came into effect in 2024, changed the way UK goods are processed on entry into EU member states, adding documentation requirements that many exporters are still not meeting consistently.

On the sanctions side, the UK now maintains its own sanctions regime under the Sanctions and Anti-Money Laundering Act 2018, administered by HMRC and the Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office. This regime does not mirror the EU or US sanctions lists exactly, which means that cross-referencing all three lists — UK, EU, and OFAC — is necessary for any company with transatlantic and European customer relationships.

Export regulations UK has also expanded in scope for dual-use goods. The UK Strategic Export Control List (UK SECL) was updated post-Brexit and continues to be amended. Products that were previously controlled under EU regulations now require UK-specific export licences in some cases. Exporters who have not reviewed their product classifications against the current UK SECL since 2021 are likely operating with outdated compliance assumptions.

## The Core International Trade Compliance Checklist for UK Exporters

Getting compliance right starts with documentation. Here is what every UK exporter shipping internationally should have in place before goods leave the facility:

**Commercial invoice:** Must include the full legal name and address of buyer and seller, HS code for each item, precise description of goods, declared value in the currency of the transaction, country of origin, and incoterms agreed. Errors or omissions in the commercial invoice are the leading cause of customs delays.

**Packing list:** Must correspond exactly to the commercial invoice — item counts, weights, dimensions, and packaging type. Discrepancies between the invoice and packing list trigger customs queries in most markets.

**Export declaration:** In the UK, this is submitted through HMRC's Customs Declaration Service (CDS). The declaration requires correct HS codes, an accurate customs value, the exporter's EORI number, the applicable customs procedure code, and, where required, a licence number.

**Certificate of origin:** Required by many markets to determine preferential duty treatment. For UK exports to markets with a UK FTA (such as the Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership — CPTPP), origin certification is mandatory to access preferential tariff rates.

**Export licence:** Required for goods on the UK SECL. Check your products against the current list annually and whenever you enter a new market or modify a product.

## Meeting the Latest UK Export Compliance Requirements by Market

*What are the latest international trade compliance requirements for exporting from the UK?* This question is asked frequently by UK manufacturers and trading companies expanding into the EU, US, and Middle East markets in 2026. The answer requires breaking it down by destination, because each market has distinct requirements.

For the EU: UK exporters no longer benefit from EU single market access. Goods entering the EU are now subject to full customs entry requirements, including HS code classification, duty assessment, VAT import obligations, and in some categories (food, pharma, chemicals) sanitary and phytosanitary checks. Importers in EU member states are responsible for customs entry, but UK exporters bear the documentation burden. Exporters should ensure they have a EU-registered importer of record in place for each EU country they ship to, rather than relying on the consignee to handle compliance.

For the US: UK goods entering the US are subject to CBP (Customs and Border Protection) requirements, including accurate HTS code classification, correct declared value, and ISF (Importer Security Filing) submission at least 24 hours before cargo loading. US tariffs on UK goods vary by category — cross-border trade rules here include Section 301 tariffs on some categories that affect competitiveness.

For the Middle East: UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar all require certificates of origin from an accredited UK body (such as a Chamber of Commerce), halal certification for food and pharmaceutical products, SASO approval for products entering Saudi Arabia, and in some cases end-user certificates for controlled goods.

## Sanctions Screening and Import Duty Compliance: What UK Exporters Miss Most

The two areas where UK exporters most commonly have gaps in their compliance programme are sanctions screening and import duty compliance — and both carry significant penalties.

On sanctions, the UK Consolidated List maintained by HMRC includes individuals, entities, and vessels designated under multiple sanctions regimes. Screening should occur at every stage where a new counterparty is introduced: initial customer onboarding, payment processing, freight forwarder appointment, and any change in beneficial ownership. Many exporters screen at the customer level but miss freight forwarder and financial intermediary screening — a known enforcement gap.

On import duty compliance, the principle of customs compliance exporters often overlook is that incoterms affect who bears the duty risk, not who causes the compliance problem. An exporter shipping DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) is legally responsible for correct customs valuation, which means that undervaluing goods on a commercial invoice to reduce the buyer's duty burden is a UK export offence, not just a destination country problem.

Actionable compliance checklist for UK exporters:

- Review your HS code classifications against the current UK tariff at least annually
- Screen all counterparties against UK, EU, and OFAC sanctions lists at onboarding and at every transaction
- Confirm whether your products appear on the UK SECL before entering any new market
- Ensure your commercial invoices include all mandatory fields as required by your destination markets
- Register with HMRC's Customs Declaration Service if you have not already — your customs agent needs your EORI
- Brief your sales and operations teams on what they can and cannot commit to without compliance review

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the UK Consolidated Sanctions List and do I need to screen against it?**
The UK Consolidated Sanctions List is the official register of individuals, entities, ships, and aircraft subject to financial sanctions under UK law, maintained by HMRC and the FCDO. All UK exporters must screen their customers, agents, freight forwarders, and financial intermediaries against this list before conducting any transaction with them. Failure to do so and inadvertently trading with a designated entity is a criminal offence under the Sanctions and Anti-Money Laundering Act 2018. The list is updated regularly and should be screened against at onboarding and at each transaction, not just annually.

**Do UK exporters need a separate export licence since Brexit?**
Yes, for goods that fall under the UK Strategic Export Control List. Post-Brexit, UK exporters can no longer use EU export licences, and the UK SECL — while similar to the EU dual-use list — has its own classifications and conditions. Products including certain electronic components, encryption technology, chemical precursors, military-grade equipment, and some industrial machinery may require an individual or open export licence from the ECJU before they can be exported. Exporters should classify their products against the current UK SECL and consult the ECJU or an export compliance consultant if any product falls within a controlled category.

**What documentation do UK exporters need to ship goods to the UAE?**
For most commercial goods, UK exporters shipping to the UAE need a commercial invoice, packing list, certificate of origin (issued by an accredited UK Chamber of Commerce and in some cases certified by the UAE Embassy), and a bill of lading or airway bill. Food and pharmaceutical products require halal certification and may need ESMA (Emirates Authority for Standardisation and Metrology) approval. Controlled goods require an end-user certificate from the UAE importer and in some cases an export licence from the ECJU. All documents may need to be attested by the UAE Embassy in London, depending on the product category and UAE customs requirements.
