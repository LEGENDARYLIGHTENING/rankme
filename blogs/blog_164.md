## FAQ

**What margin do food and beverage distributors typically take?**
Margins vary significantly depending on the product category, the level of service provided, and the specific retail channel. Generally, broadline distributors require a margin ranging from 15% to 25%. However, Direct Store Delivery (DSD) networks, which physically place products on the shelf and manage local inventory levels, often demand higher margins, typically between 25% and 35%, due to their increased labor and logistical costs.

**Do distributors help with marketing and brand awareness?**
Most traditional broadline distributors focus strictly on logistics—moving boxes from warehouses to retail backrooms—and offer zero consumer marketing support. However, specialized DSD networks and premium intermediaries often provide localized merchandising, end-cap setup, and in-store promotional support. Ultimately, the brand remains primarily responsible for driving consumer demand and pulling the product off the retail shelf through dedicated marketing efforts.

**What is a chargeback, and how does it affect brand profitability?**
A chargeback is a fee deducted by the distributor from the brand's invoice to cover specific operational or promotional costs. Common chargebacks include fees for warehousing slow-moving inventory, participating in mandatory retailer promotions, slotting fees for new shelf placement, or penalties for delayed shipments. Brands must rigorously model these hidden costs during contract negotiations, as unexpected chargebacks can completely destroy a product's wholesale profitability.
