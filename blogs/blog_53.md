## What a Strong Vetting Process for Contract Manufacturers Looks Like

Manufacturing partner vetting is where most sourcing deals fail — not in negotiation, not in production, but in the qualification stage where critical information is either not gathered or not verified.

Start with capability documentation. A credible electronics contract manufacturing partner should provide ISO 9001 or ISO 13485 certification (for medical devices), IPC-A-610 workmanship standards, component sourcing documentation, and equipment specifications for the assembly lines relevant to your product. Ask which specific production lines your product would run on. Generic factory capability sheets are not enough — you want line-level specifications.

Request a factory audit, either in person or through a third-party inspection firm. For Asia-based manufacturers, inspection firms such as QIMA, Bureau Veritas, or SGS provide pre-audit, production monitoring, and final inspection services. Skipping the audit because travel is difficult is not a valid reason — third-party audits exist precisely for this situation.

Reference checks matter more in contract manufacturing than in most B2B relationships because the consequences of a bad choice are embedded in your product unit economics for years. Ask for three references from clients in your product category and contact all of them. Ask about defect rates, how the manufacturer handles quality issues mid-run, and whether they communicate proactively when problems emerge.

## Structuring the Contract Manufacturing Relationship for Scale

Getting into production is one challenge. Structuring the relationship for long-term scale is a different one. The companies that manage outsource manufacturing relationships well treat the contract manufacturer as a strategic partner, not a transactional vendor. That means sharing product roadmaps, giving forecast visibility, and building in performance review cycles — not just chasing deliveries.

Key elements of a well-structured contract manufacturing agreement:

- **IP and NDA terms:** Define exactly what proprietary information is shared, who owns it, and what restrictions apply to the manufacturer regarding use, sublicensing, and competing clients
- **Component sourcing approval:** Specify approved component suppliers and require written approval for any substitutions — component substitution is the single most common cause of quality failures in electronics contract manufacturing
- **Quality standards and defect tolerance:** Define Acceptable Quality Limits (AQL) by defect category, inspection stages, and consequences for exceeding tolerance thresholds
- **Lead time and penalty clauses:** Set realistic lead times with built-in buffers, and define escalation steps (not automatic penalties) for deviations
- **Tooling and mould ownership:** Clarify who owns tooling created for your product and ensure buyout terms are specified in case you need to change manufacturers

Actionable takeaways for product companies and procurement teams:

- Never share product design files before a signed NDA and IP protection agreement
- Always audit the production line for your product specifically, not the factory generally
- Build a supplier scorecard and review it quarterly — not just after a problem occurs
- Have a second-source manufacturer identified before you need them, not after a crisis
- Get payment terms tied to inspection milestones, not calendar dates
- Include a clear exit clause with IP return provisions in every manufacturing agreement

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I protect my product IP when working with a contract manufacturer in Asia?**
IP protection starts before any design files are shared. Execute a mutual NDA that is governed by a jurisdiction where you can enforce it — Hong Kong law is common for Asia-based manufacturing agreements and provides more predictable enforcement than mainland China law. Include specific provisions covering design files, firmware, tooling ownership, and restrictions on producing similar products for competitors. Register trademarks and relevant patents in the manufacturing country before entering production. These steps do not guarantee zero risk, but they establish the legal framework needed to act if a violation occurs.

**What is the typical minimum order quantity for electronics contract manufacturing in Asia?**
MOQs vary significantly by manufacturer and product complexity. For PCB assembly and electronics production, MOQs for prototype or low-volume runs typically start at 100 to 500 units with smaller Shenzhen-based manufacturers. Mid-tier manufacturers typically require 1,000 to 5,000 units per run. Enterprise-tier manufacturers like Foxconn operate at volumes well above 10,000 units. Some manufacturers offer NPI (New Product Introduction) programmes specifically for startups that allow lower initial volumes with a committed scale-up schedule. Always negotiate NPI terms before committing to a production partner.

**How long does it take to vet and onboard a contract manufacturing partner in Asia?**
A thorough vetting process typically takes 8 to 16 weeks from initial outreach to production start. This includes 2 to 3 weeks for RFQ and capability review, 2 to 4 weeks for factory audit and reference verification, 2 to 4 weeks for agreement negotiation, and 2 to 6 weeks for tooling, first article inspection (FAI), and production approval. Rushing this process is the single most common cause of first-production quality failures. Budget the time correctly from the start of your project planning.
